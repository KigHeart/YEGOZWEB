Central Thesis

The history of transistor scaling is a history of progressively recovering electrostatic gate control over the channel. Every architecture change — FinFET, GAAFet, CFET, 2D materials — solves the same underlying equation: reducing the natural length scale λ to suppress sub-threshold leakage, drain-induced barrier lowering (DIBL), and quantum tunnelling. The implications for AI chips are architectural, not merely parametric.

The Central Equation — Why Everything Changes

Every transistor architecture in history can be understood through one equation — the characteristic length λ, which governs how far the drain electric field penetrates into the channel:

Eq. 5 — Planar MOSFET
λ = √(ε_Si/ε_ox · t_Si · t_ox)
Short-channel effects become severe when gate length L_g ≲ 5λ. Every architecture reduces λ.

For GAAFet nanosheets (TSMC N2), this becomes:

Eq. 15 — Nanosheet GAA
λ_NS ≈ √(ε_Si/ε_ox · T_NS/4 · t_ox) ≈ 1.6 nm at N2
The factor of 4 (vs 2 for FinFET) reflects 4-sided gate control. Enables L_g as short as 8 nm.

And for 2D materials (the Angstrom Era):

Eq. 26 — 2D Material (MoS₂)
λ_2D ≈ √(ε_ch/C_gate · t_ch) ≈ 0.9 nm for MoS₂ (t_ch = 0.65 nm)
Sub-1 nm λ enables true Angstrom-Era gate lengths while maintaining SCE suppression (L_g/λ > 5).

Architecture Evolution — The Full Progression

Each transistor generation solves a specific failure mode of its predecessor:

Planar MOSFET
Pre-2011 · >22nm

Gate on top only. At short channels, drain field penetrates entire body. SCE uncontrollable below ~100nm.

FinFET
2011–2025 · 22nm→3nm

3-sided gate on vertical silicon fin. λ_DG ≈ 2.3nm. Fails below 3nm: fin collapse, bottom leakage, quantised current.

GAAFet Nanosheet
2025–2028 · N2/A16

4-sided gate wraps all surfaces. λ_NS ≈ 1.6nm. Continuous W_NS tuning. TSMC N2: +15% perf, −30% power vs N3E.

Forksheet FET
2029–2030 · A10

n/p separated by thin dielectric wall. Eliminates n-p spacing without full CFET challenge. 5% lower RO delay.

CFET
2031+ · A7/A3

n-FET stacked directly on p-FET. Eliminates n-p gap from cell height. 10–20% area reduction. imec: 79% yield at 18nm gate.

2D Materials
2034+ · A3/A2

MoS₂ (n) + WSe₂ (p) channels. Atomically thin (0.65nm). λ < 1nm. IEDM 2024: I_on = 451 µA/µm, I_on/I_off > 10⁹.

Present AI Chips — Transistor-Level Analysis

NVIDIA Blackwell (B200): 208B transistors at TSMC N4P (4nm FinFET — paradoxically pre-GAAFet). Two GB100 dies fused via 10 TB/s NV-HBI interconnect. FP4 Tensor Cores deliver ~18 PFLOPS. Power density of 1,250 W/cm² mandates liquid cooling.

Roofline Model — GPU vs LPU
P_attain = min(P_peak, B × I)
For LLM inference at batch=1: I ≈ 1–2 FLOP/byte. A 3.9 PFLOP GPU is limited to ~5 TFLOPS — just 0.13% of peak. This is why Groq wins at inference.

Groq LPU: ~220 MB on-chip SRAM (~10B transistors in 6T cells). Static compiler scheduling achieves η_util ≈ 0.99 vs 0.1–0.3 for GPU warp scheduling. Result: >1,600 tokens/second vs 10–30 for GPU at batch=1.

The AI Chip of 2035

The AI chip of 2035 will not be a faster GPU. Based on the transistor roadmap, it will be a heterogeneous, photonically-interconnected, memristively-augmented system-in-package operating on principles architecturally orthogonal to CUDA:

Compute Die A — Dense FP4/FP8 training on TSMC A14 GAAFet. Compute Die B — Deterministic LPU-style inference on SRAM-optimised N3. Memory Die — HBM4 for bandwidth + capacity. Photonic I/O Die — 400+ Tb/s optical I/O at 5 pJ/bit. Neuro Die — Memristive SNN cores at <1 fJ/MAC, 10× more efficient than digital CMOS.

All integrated on a silicon or glass interposer with <1 µm bump pitch and >1 TB/s die-to-die bandwidth.
